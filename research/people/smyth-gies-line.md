### 1b. The Neustädter Immigration (Historical Context)

🟢 **HIGH CONFIDENCE** — Well-documented historical event

The first organized German immigration to Detroit began in **1830**, when a man nicknamed "Victor" (the "Hessian Columbus") returned to Neustadt, Kurhessen, with enthusiastic reports about Detroit. The migrants traveled by wagon from Neustadt to the port of **Bremen**, then sailed to New York, and made their way to Detroit. This movement brought entire families and established the foundation of Detroit's German-speaking community. A centennial publication was produced in 1930 by the Neustadter Kirmess Committee of Detroit.

Key figures of this immigration include Johann Heinrich Gies and Andreas Schmittdiel. These immigrants became foundational members of Detroit's German community, contributing to the establishment of **St. Mary's Roman Catholic Church** (founded 1834, the first German Catholic parish in Detroit).

**Sources:**
- Centennial publication: "The Hundredth Anniversary of the Arrival in Detroit of the First Organized Immigration from Germany" (1930) — https://familienforschung-neustadt-hessen.de/wp-content/uploads/2021/06/2015-03-26-Booklet-Hundredth-Anniversary-of-Neustadt-Immigrants-at-Michigan.pdf

## 4. Key Research Questions — Analysis

### Question 1: What rebellion was William Smyth involved in?

**Analysis:** 🟡 MEDIUM CONFIDENCE — Analysis based on timeline and historical context

The family tradition states William Smyth "damned the Queen" and was forced to flee Ireland. He married Mary Elizabeth Gies in **1835**, meaning he was already in Canada/the Chatham area by that year. This creates a critical chronological issue:

| Rebellion | Date | Compatible? | Notes |
|-----------|------|-------------|-------|
| **1798 Irish Rebellion** | 1798 | ❌ Too early | William would need to have been born c. 1770s–1780s; his son Henry was born 1841 |
| **Tithe War** | 1831–1836 | ✅ **Most likely** | Irish Catholic resistance to Anglican tithes; violent in 1831–33; people were arrested and some fled |
| **Upper Canada Rebellion** | 1837–1838 | ❌ | William was already in Canada married by 1835 |
| **Young Ireland Rebellion** | 1848 | ❌ Too late | He was settled in Chatham by this time |

**Most probable scenario:** William fled Ireland in the early 1830s during the **Tithe War** (1831–1836), a period of widespread agrarian resistance by Irish Catholics against mandatory payments to the established Anglican Church. "Damning the Queen" may be a family retelling that conflated the Crown/monarch with the broader establishment — note that Queen Victoria did not ascend until June 1837; before that, King William IV was on the throne (1830–1837). The phrase may have originally been "damned the King" or simply "damned the Crown" and evolved in family retelling.

**Alternative:** William may have been involved in more general Irish agrarian agitation (Whiteboy movement, Ribbonism, or other secret society activity) in the 1820s–early 1830s and fled to avoid arrest.

### Question 2: What was Mr. Kitchen's first name?

**Finding:** 🔴 **UNKNOWN** — No records found

Isabella Smyth's first husband, Mr. Kitchen, who married her c. 1863 and died c. 1870, remains unidentified. His first name does not appear in any publicly accessible records online. Only the family's handwritten chart mentions this marriage. Recommended follow-up:
- Search Ontario death registrations (1869–1870) for a "Kitchen" in Kent County
- Search Chatham cemetery records for Kitchen burials in the late 1860s–1870
- Check Chatham city directories from the 1860s

### Question 3: Can we find the Gies family in Hesse, Germany records?

**Finding:** 🟡 PARTIALLY — The well-documented Gies family from Neustadt/Hessen does NOT match

The famous Johann Heinrich Gies (1798–1865) from Neustadt, Hessen, who arrived in Detroit in 1832 and married Maria Anna Schmittdiel, is **well-documented** in WikiTree, Ancestry, Find a Grave, and HistoryDetroit.com. However, his wife, children, and arrival date do **not** match the family records for the Montgomery ancestor "Henry Gies of Hessen" who married Mary Catherine Kuhn.

This means either:
1. The Montgomery family's "Henry Gies" is a **different individual** from the same Neustadt/Hessen immigration wave
2. There is an error in the family tradition about the wife's name or children
3. The Henry Gies in the family records may have been from a different town in Hessen

**Recommended follow-up:**
- Search the 1930 Centennial Publication of the Neustädter Immigration for additional Gies families
- Search Hessen State Archives (Hessisches Staatsarchiv) emigration lists
- Search the Michigan Germanic Genealogical Society records
- Check Detroit church records (St. Mary's RC Church, founded 1834) for Gies/Kuhn marriages

## 5. Discrepancies & Open Questions

### 5a. Thomas Holmes Death Date: 1892 vs. 1896

The Montgomery family's preliminary tree records Thomas Holmes's death as **1892**. However, Find a Grave memorial #85191386 records his death as **April 11, 1896**. This is a **4-year discrepancy** that should be resolved through Ontario death registration records.

- Family records: d. 1892
- Find a Grave: d. 1896 (Thomas Holmes, 1817–1896, Maple Leaf Cemetery)

**Source:** https://www.findagrave.com/memorial/85191386/thomas-holmes

### 5b. Henry Gies Arrival: 1834 vs. 1832

The family records say Henry Gies came to Detroit in **1834**. The documented Neustädter Immigration began in **1830**, with a significant wave in **1832**. If the family's Henry Gies was part of this migration, 1834 could represent a later wave or a memory error. St. Mary's Church was founded in **1834**, which may have confused the immigration date with the church founding in family memory.
